\section{Inner and outer Casimir witnesses}

To make the argument self-contained, we briefly recall the geometric objects that supply the inner and outer Casimir factors. (Full technical details appear in the dynamic-mass-spectrum synthesis paper; here we give only the minimal physical and algebraic content required for the present claim.)

A \emph{Hopf shell} in the discrete setting is an integrable three-dimensional complex on the octonion carrier, equipped with a curvature imprint $\alpha_n$ and a torsion matrix derived from the contact Beltrami operator on that shell. These are the finite, machine-checkable counterparts of the nested Hopf fibrations in Nielsen's topological construction.

The \emph{inner witness} (Lean structure \texttt{exampleNonFactorableWitnessForIntegrableHopfShells}) packages exactly three such shells, with winding numbers $n=1,2,3$. Each shell carries its own torsion matrix obtained from the canonical phase-lift construction on the carrier. The defining property of the witness is \emph{non-factorability}: the three torsion matrices cannot be simultaneously block-diagonalised on any proper subspace of the eight-dimensional carrier. The structure is irreducible; the full octonion carrier participates as a single unit. 

The \emph{inner contact surfaces} of these three shells are the locus of the trapped Casimir effect. The geometric factor generated on these surfaces (the trapping selection functional, modulated by the curvature primitive) is what the framework identifies as the source of binding energy and of the heavy stabilisation gap. In the Lean implementation this factor is explicitly labelled as supplying the ``trapped, binding, heavy/gluonic'' contribution.

The \emph{outer witness} (Lean \texttt{outerShellNeutrinoFluctuationWitness}) is the corresponding datum on the first shell beyond the reference lock-in surface.
